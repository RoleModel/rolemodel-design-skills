# UI/UX Audit Practice — Team Guide

## How We Approach Partner Re-Engagement Audits

## Why We're Doing This

We have a real opportunity with some of our long-standing partners — products we may have launched years ago that could genuinely benefit from what we can do today. The goal isn't to walk in with a list of problems. It's to show up with energy and a compelling vision for what these products could become.

This is a practice we're building out deliberately. Done well, these audits should be exciting for our partners — not a critique of past decisions, but an honest look at where things stand and a motivating picture of what's possible. They should also be efficient for us. That's where our AI-accelerated workflow comes in.

## What We're Evaluating — Four Lenses

### 1. Experience Gaps
These are the moments where users have to work harder than they should. Confusing navigation, flows that don't match how people think about a task, forms that ask for too much. Keep asking: **"Where does this product fight the user?"**

### 2. Visual & Brand Coherence
Design inconsistency accumulates quietly over time. Different button treatments, mismatched type scales, color usage that's drifted — these erode trust even when users can't articulate why. Frame as: *"there's an opportunity to make this feel like one cohesive, modern product again."*

### 3. Modernization Moments
High-visibility improvements that have become table stakes since the original design — accessibility compliance (WCAG), mobile responsiveness, dark mode, a proper component system. Partners respond well because these are concrete and familiar.

### 4. Strategic Opportunities
Where is the product constrained by old design decisions that are actually solvable now? What could it do that it doesn't today? This section should feel generative — a glimpse of a v-next, not a punch list.

## The "Then / Now / Next" Narrative Frame

- **Then** — Honor the original work and the context it was built in. What were the constraints? What did it accomplish? Say this genuinely.
- **Now** — Name what's changed. User expectations, technology, the competitive landscape. This isn't criticism; it's context.
- **Next** — The opportunity. This is where the energy goes.

## Deliverable Structure

### Executive Summary
One page, shareable upward. Plain language, no design jargon. What's the core opportunity, what are the 2–3 things we'd do, and what's the rough shape of the impact. One strong narrative paragraph and a few clear callouts.

### Findings
Organized by **theme** (the 4 lenses), **not severity**. Each finding pairs *"what we observed"* with *"what this means for users."* Annotated screenshots throughout. Grounded in behavior, not opinion.

### Opportunities
Where wireframes, UI design sketches, and prototypes live. Visually rich, relatively concise. Show a direction and make people lean forward.

### What's Next
An explicit invitation. What could a phase one look like? What questions would we want to explore together? Leave it open — starting a conversation, not closing a sale.

## Key Principles

- Not a bug report. A magazine.
- Findings pair "what we observed" with "what this means for users."
- Never say "this looks outdated." Say "there's an opportunity to make this feel like one cohesive product again."
- The clickable prototype is the single biggest differentiator.
- Keep scope ruthlessly tight: one flow, one improvement, built to feel right.
- Respect the fact that partners are emotionally invested in the work.
